Research

Research Overview

From fish feeding to salamander locomotion, I am captivated by how animals interact with their environment and how ecology influences their morphology. My research integrates functional morphology, biomechanics, kinematics, phylogenetic comparative methods, and natural history to study patterns of morphological and functional diversification. My science is motivated by my fascination with the natural world, and so I am not tied to any one system. However, I do have a particular fondness towards fishes due to their immense diversity and how they can be used to answer outstanding questions in the field of ecology and evolution. Learn more about some of the themes of my research below.


Salamander locomotion and biomechanics

For a large part of my PhD, I am studying how salamanders are adapted for moving between mediums. For example, do terrestrial salamanders have stronger limb bones than aquatic ones to withstand the habitual effects of gravity? To that end, I borrowed techniques from mechanical engineering and designed new software to estimate limb bone stiffness from CT scans. Soon, I will start to investigate the behavioral and morphological adaptations of arboreal and climbing salamanders as they left the ground and took to the trees. So stay tuned.

Suction-based adhesion in fishes

Many fish families have evolved modified pelvic girdles and fin rays that form suction discs on their bellies. These biological suction cups are highly effective and can stick to both rough and wet surfaces. I have studied how well biological and bio-inspired suction cups perform on challenging substrates. More recently, we have also compared how much force it takes to dislodge different species (clingfish, lumpsuckers, and snailfish) in hopes of untangling the complicated relationship between disc morphology, ecology, and adhesive performance.

Fish feeding morphology and evolution

Some fish feed on truly bizarre prey from the scales, mucous, and parasites of other fishes; to microscopic invertebrates living in the sand! Many of my collaborations center around understanding what kinds of morphological, biomechanical, or behavioral adaptations facilitate these unusual trophic transitions. As of late, I am currently studying how one species of fish feeds on sea anemones. Because anemones are difficult to chew and have harmful stinging cells, we hypothesize that this cnidarian nibbler may have both behavioral and morphological adaptations.

Anole ecomorphology

Caribbean anole lizards have repeatedly evolved groups of species (ecomorphs) with similar ecologies and morphologies on four separate islands. While the ecomorphology of Caribbean anoles has been well studied, relatively little is known about Central and South America anoles. As an NSF REU intern at the Smithsonian's NMNH, I tested whether mainland species could be classified using the same ecomorph scheme (it's complicated). Now as a Grad Student Fellow at the NMNH, I have some smaller ongoing collaborations on anole ecomorphology.
